## What complexity bias means

Complexity bias is the tendency to favour complex explanations, solutions, and strategies over simpler ones - and to mistrust simplicity as somehow inadequate, naive, or suspicious. When faced with a choice between a straightforward account and an elaborate one, many people instinctively gravitate toward the more complex version, feeling that complexity signals depth, sophistication, and thoroughness.

This is not the same as recognising that some problems are genuinely complex. Many are. Complexity bias is the error of adding unnecessary complexity - of assuming that a simple explanation must be missing something, or that a straightforward solution cannot possibly work for a problem that feels difficult.

The bias operates in both directions. People over-complicate their own thinking (adding unnecessary steps, conditions, and qualifications) and they over-value complexity in others (preferring a convoluted analysis to a clear one, trusting a jargon-heavy expert over a plain-speaking one). In both cases, complexity is treated as a proxy for quality, when [Occam's razor](/occams-razor/) suggests the opposite: all else being equal, the simpler explanation is more likely to be correct.

## How complexity bias works

Complexity bias is driven by several interacting psychological tendencies.

### Complexity feels like thoroughness

There is an intuitive link between complexity and completeness. A complex explanation seems like it accounts for more variables. A complex solution seems like it has considered more possibilities. A complex argument seems like it has been more carefully constructed. This intuition is sometimes correct - but it is often wrong. Complexity can be a sign of thoroughness, or it can be a sign of confusion, over-fitting, or the failure to identify the essential from the incidental.

### Simplicity feels suspicious

When a problem feels complex, a simple answer triggers suspicion. "It can't be that simple" is one of the most common expressions of complexity bias. The feeling is that if a simple answer were correct, someone would have found it already. This reasoning ignores the possibility that the simple answer was always there and was overlooked precisely because it felt too easy.

### Expertise creates complexity bias

Paradoxically, expertise can intensify complexity bias. The more you know about a domain, the more you are aware of its nuances, edge cases, and complications. This awareness can make you resistant to simple explanations that a novice might accept without hesitation. Sometimes this resistance is justified - the expert sees genuine complications. Sometimes it is complexity bias - the expert adds unnecessary nuance to a problem that has a straightforward core.

## Complexity bias in everyday life

Complexity bias shapes thinking in contexts from personal decisions to global policy.

### Health and wellness

The wellness industry thrives on complexity bias. A simple health message - eat a varied diet, exercise regularly, sleep enough - feels too ordinary to be a complete answer. The result is an endless market for complicated supplements, intricate diets, elaborate exercise regimens, and multistep routines, each of which offers the appealing complexity that the simple advice lacks. The simple advice is, in most cases, more evidence-based and more effective.

### Conspiracy theories

Complexity bias is a key engine of conspiracy thinking. When a major event occurs - a political assassination, a pandemic, a financial crisis - the straightforward explanation often feels inadequate to the magnitude of the event. Surely something this significant must have a complex, hidden cause. This feeling draws people toward elaborate explanatory frameworks involving secret coordination, hidden actors, and multi-layered cover-ups.

The connection to [apophenia](/apophenia/) is direct: complexity bias makes you hungry for intricate patterns, and apophenia provides them. Each new coincidence, each suggestive detail, each ambiguous fact is woven into the complex explanation, creating a narrative that feels more complete and more satisfying than the simple one - even when it is wrong.

### Business and technology

In business, complexity bias manifests as over-engineering: building elaborate processes, adding unnecessary features, creating multi-layered strategies when simpler approaches would be more effective. The assumption is that a complex problem demands a complex solution. Often, the most effective solution is the simplest one that addresses the core issue - a principle that is easy to state and difficult to follow when complexity feels more serious and professional.

### Communication

Complexity bias affects how we evaluate communication. A jargon-heavy explanation is often perceived as more authoritative than a plain-language one, even when they convey the same information. Research has shown that unnecessary complexity in academic writing can increase perceived credibility - readers assume that if something is hard to understand, it must be sophisticated.

This is one reason why [weasel words](/weasel-words/) and unnecessarily complex language persist in professional communication. They exploit the bias that equates complexity with quality.

## Why complexity bias is so persistent

Complexity bias persists because it serves psychological needs beyond accuracy.

### Complexity signals intelligence

Preferring complex explanations signals that you are sophisticated enough to handle them. Accepting a simple explanation can feel intellectually lazy. This social pressure toward complexity is strongest in professional and academic environments, where appearing to grasp subtle distinctions is rewarded.

### Simple answers feel dismissive

When someone is worried about a complex situation, a simple answer can feel dismissive of their concern. "Just do X" can feel patronising, even when X is the correct answer. Complexity bias is partly a social phenomenon - we reach for complex answers because simple ones do not seem to take the problem seriously enough.

## How to counter complexity bias

The goal is not to force simplicity where complexity is warranted, but to stop adding complexity where it is not.

### Start with the simplest explanation

Use [Occam's razor](/occams-razor/) as a starting point. The simplest explanation consistent with the evidence is not always correct, but it is the right place to begin. Add complexity only when the simple explanation fails to account for the evidence.

### Ask what can be removed

When evaluating a plan, strategy, or explanation, ask what could be removed without losing anything essential. If the answer is "a lot," you may be dealing with unnecessary complexity.

### Separate complexity from quality

Train yourself to notice when you are using complexity as a shortcut for quality. A clear, simple explanation that accounts for the evidence is superior to a complex one that does the same thing with more steps. The goal of thinking is understanding, not elaboration.

## Complexity bias and the wider web of thinking

Complexity bias connects to [Occam's razor](/occams-razor/) (its direct counterbalance), [apophenia](/apophenia/) (which provides the patterns complexity craves), [first principles thinking](/first-principles-thinking/) (which strips problems to their essentials), and [confirmation bias](/confirmation-bias/) (which sustains complex explanations once adopted). Recognising complexity bias is recognising that your instinct for depth can sometimes pull you away from the truth rather than toward it.
